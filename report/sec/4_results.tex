\section{Experiments and Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

After cold-start filtering (at least 5 interactions per user and 3 users per
song) the modelling dataset contains 284{,}864 users and 6{,}054 tracks with
their full jSymbolic feature vectors and knowledge-graph context. Interactions
are partitioned with the user-level stratified split of \cref{sec:eval-protocol}:
1{,}943{,}681 training, 348{,}054 validation, and 559{,}806 test interactions,
stratified by primary genre, release decade, key, mode, and tempo class.
All models are trained on identical training interactions and evaluated on the
held-out test interactions; reproducibility is fixed with seed~42. Hyperparameters
are those listed in \cref{sec:methodology} and were selected on the validation split
only.

\subsection{Quantitative Comparison}

\subsection{Discussion}

The completed baselines collectively illustrate the central tension of the
task. \textbf{MostPopular} achieves the highest hit-rate on short lists
(0.184 at $K$=10) but is nearly useless as a discovery tool: it covers only
0.2\% of the 6{,}054-song catalogue and assigns the highest popularity bias
(0.628), confirming that it amplifies existing listening patterns rather than
enabling exploration.

\textbf{KNN-CF} ($k$=6) demonstrates that collaborative filtering already
breaks the popularity trap effectively: NDCG@10 rises slightly above
MostPopular (0.106 vs.\ 0.098) while coverage jumps to 91.5\% and popularity
bias drops to 0.133, producing an Overall score of 0.420---more than three
times the popularity baseline (0.134). The optimal neighbourhood size of 6 is
notably small, suggesting that tight, high-quality neighbourhoods outperform
broad ones for this dataset.

\textbf{XGBoost-Hybrid} reveals the limits of content-only candidate
generation: despite incorporating musical features (key, mode, tempo, genre,
instruments) and 128-dimensional autoencoder embeddings, the model's ranking
accuracy (NDCG@10~=~0.015) falls far below both collaborative baselines. The
low accuracy likely reflects the mismatch between content similarity (which
the AE embeddings capture) and the actual listening preferences of individual
users, which are poorly approximated by a mean-pool user profile over only
3{,}000 training users. Notably, the model achieves the lowest popularity bias
of any model (0.032), confirming that pure content-based retrieval naturally
avoids recommending well-known tracks.

These three points of reference frame the expected contribution of the
\textbf{HGT}: the graph-relational structure should enable message passing to
carry both collaborative and content signals simultaneously, potentially closing
the accuracy gap that the content-only hybrid exhibits while preserving the
diversity improvements over the popularity baseline. \TODO{Update this
