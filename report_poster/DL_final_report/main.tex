\begin{abstract}
We present a deep-learning pipeline for content- and preference-aware music
recommendation built on a heterogeneous music knowledge graph (KG). Starting from
the Lakh MIDI and Million Song datasets, we align 7{,}013 tracks, compress their
1{,}526-dimensional symbolic feature vectors to a 128-dimensional embedding with a
fully connected autoencoder (final MSE~0.616), and pre-train KG embeddings
(RotatE, ComplEx) over 807{,}955 relational triples to initialise every entity
type. A Heterogeneous Graph Transformer (HGT) is then trained with an
intensity-weighted listwise loss augmented by logit adjustment for popularity
debiasing. Against three baselines (MostPopular, KNN collaborative filtering, and
an XGBoost content--behaviour hybrid), the HGT reaches Recall@10~0.105,
NDCG@10~0.097 and 79.7\% catalogue coverage with the lowest popularity bias of all
models (0.053), giving an Overall Score of 0.399 --- second only to KNN-CF (0.421)
and significantly ahead of the XGBoost hybrid (0.336) and the popularity baseline
(0.148). Relative to our Milestone~2 report, evaluating the HGT with message
passing consistent with training (undirected) resolves the directed-evaluation
artefact that had previously collapsed its scores, and a retuned architecture plus
RotatE initialisation now outperform the earlier configuration on the composite
objective. We further analyse
the learned latent space (20 emergent user archetypes) and produce per-edge
attention rationales that explain individual recommendations.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Music recommendation is dominated by collaborative filtering (CF), which infers
taste from interaction overlap alone. CF favours popular content and is blind to
\emph{why} a user likes a track: a listener of Baroque counterpoint and a listener
of jazz both interact with mainstream pop, so a purely behavioural model conflates
two distinct preferences. We ask whether a deep model that reasons over explicit
musical structure --- tracks, artists, genres, keys, tempo classes, instruments,
and users connected by typed relations --- can recommend more diverse and
content-grounded items while remaining competitive on ranking quality.

\textbf{Plan.} Our approach is an end-to-end deep-learning pipeline (Fig.~\ref{fig:pipeline}).
We (i)~integrate the Lakh MIDI Dataset~\cite{raffel2016lakh} and Million Song
Dataset~\cite{msd2011} into a music KG; (ii)~compress high-dimensional symbolic
descriptors with a \emph{symbolic-feature autoencoder} and pre-train
\emph{KG embeddings} (RotatE~\cite{sun2019rotate},
ComplEx~\cite{trouillon2016complex}) to initialise all node types;
(iii)~train a \emph{Heterogeneous Graph Transformer}~\cite{hu2020hgt} with a
popularity-debiased listwise loss; and (iv)~evaluate the model family against three
baselines on a ranking-plus-diversity metric suite, complemented by latent-space
and attention-based interpretability analyses.

This document is the final Deep Learning report. It consolidates the proposal and
the two milestone reports and updates them to match the work actually done. The
single most consequential change since Milestone~2 is that the HGT --- previously
crippled by an evaluation-protocol mismatch --- now ranks second among all models,
with the lowest popularity bias and strong catalogue coverage, once inference is
made consistent with training and the architecture is retuned. Our contributions are: (1)~a deep representation stack coupling a
symbolic autoencoder, transductive KG embeddings, and a heterogeneous GNN;
(2)~an intensity-weighted listwise loss with logit adjustment that exposes a
tunable ranking-versus-diversity trade-off; (3)~a controlled study of node
initialisation, debiasing strength, and architecture; and (4)~interpretable
recommendation rationales grounded in HGT attention.

\section{Problem Statement}
\label{sec:problem}

\paragraph{Task.} Given a user's listening history, recommend a ranked list of
$K$ unseen tracks. We treat this as implicit-feedback top-$K$ ranking over a fixed
catalogue of 6{,}054 modelled songs and 284{,}864 users.

\paragraph{Data.} We use the Lakh MIDI Dataset (symbolic MIDI aligned to audio via
chroma DTW) and the Million Song Dataset (audio-derived scalars, artist/genre
metadata, and the Echo Nest Taste Profile of $\sim$48M user--song--playcount
triplets). After mismatch removal, an inner join to the LMD-matched tracks, and a
5-interaction cold-start floor, 3{,}048{,}959 interactions remain over 285{,}037
users and 7{,}227 songs; 7{,}013 tracks yield valid symbolic features and form the
modelling set (Sec.~\ref{sec:data}).

\paragraph{Expected result.} A model that exploits musical content and relational
structure should (a)~match or approach CF ranking quality while (b)~substantially
improving catalogue coverage and lowering popularity bias, and (c)~offering
human-readable explanations --- properties that popularity-driven CF cannot
provide.

\paragraph{Evaluation.} A user-level stratified 70/10/20 split (by primary genre
and release decade) preserves popularity and content distributions across folds;
KG structure and track features are visible to all folds and only interactions are
partitioned. We report per-user Recall, Precision, F1, HitRate, MRR, and NDCG at
$K\in\{5,10,20,50,100\}$, plus two catalogue-level metrics --- coverage
$\mathrm{Cov}=|\bigcup_u R_u|/|\mathcal{I}|$ and popularity bias PB@$K$ --- fused
into a composite
\begin{equation}\label{eq:overall}
  \mathrm{Overall@}K = 0.6\,\mathrm{NDCG@}K + 0.2\,\mathrm{Cov} + 0.2(1-\mathrm{PB@}K),
\end{equation}
which rewards accurate \emph{and} diverse recommendation and serves as the HGT
checkpoint criterion. Significance is assessed with paired Wilcoxon signed-rank,
$t$-tests, and bootstrap confidence intervals over a shared 284{,}864-user
evaluation set.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\textbf{Collaborative and neural CF.} User--user/item--item similarity and matrix
factorisation, often optimised with BPR~\cite{rendle2009bpr}, dominate implicit
feedback; Neural CF~\cite{he2017ncf} replaces the inner product with an MLP. These
methods are content-agnostic and amplify popularity bias --- the gap our model
targets. We use KNN-CF and a popularity baseline as references.

\textbf{Content-based and hybrid.} jSymbolic~\cite{mckay2018jsymbolic} and
music21~\cite{music21docs} extract hundreds of symbolic descriptors from MIDI;
their high dimensionality and redundancy motivate autoencoder compression before
recommendation. Our XGBoost hybrid retrieves on autoencoder embeddings and
re-ranks with a gradient-boosted LTR model, isolating the value of graph structure.

\textbf{KG-enhanced recommendation.} KGCN~\cite{wang2019kgcn} and
KGAT~\cite{wang2019kgat} propagate KG semantics into item representations;
LightGCN~\cite{he2020lightgcn} shows that minimal propagation suffices for
homogeneous user--item graphs; Liu et al.~\cite{liu2024kgmusic} combine KG features
with multi-task learning for music. We differ by building the KG from scratch, by
using KG embeddings as \emph{node initialisers} for a heterogeneous GNN rather than
as a separate aggregation module, and by injecting 1{,}526-dimensional symbolic
content the KG structure cannot capture.

\textbf{Heterogeneous GNNs and KG embeddings.} The Heterogeneous Graph
Transformer~\cite{hu2020hgt} learns per-(source,edge,target)-type transformation
and attention, preserving typed structure and exposing inspectable attention.
Complex-valued KG embeddings RotatE~\cite{sun2019rotate} and
ComplEx~\cite{trouillon2016complex} model asymmetric and compositional relations;
we train them on the non-interaction triples and use them to initialise every
entity type, including structural nodes (genres, keys, instruments) that have no
other numeric representation.

\paragraph{Alignment.} MIDI files are matched to MSD tracks using precomputed
chroma DTW similarities~\cite{raffel2016lakh}; we keep matches with score
$\geq 0.55$ and deduplicate, yielding $\sim$19k unique tracks.

\paragraph{Interaction filtering.} Raw Taste Profile interactions
(48{,}373{,}586) are cleaned in three stages (Tab.~\ref{tab:filtering}): MSD
mismatch IDs are removed, interactions are inner-joined to the LMD-matched track
set, and users with fewer than five remaining interactions are dropped to mitigate
cold-start, leaving 3{,}048{,}959 interactions over 285{,}037 users and 7{,}227
songs.

\paragraph{Symbolic features.} jSymbolic and music21 are applied to all MIDI
files; 7{,}013 tracks succeed, each described by 1{,}526 numerical descriptors
spanning pitch, melody, harmony, rhythm, dynamics, texture, and instrumentation.
Features are standardised before autoencoding.

\paragraph{Knowledge graph.} Tracks, performances, artists, genres, instruments,
keys, modes, tempo classes, and decades become typed nodes; user listening events
become \texttt{user}$\to$\texttt{track} edges. The populated graph contains
$1.9\!\times\!10^{7}$ triples; the entity composition is 285{,}037 listeners,
7{,}013 tracks/performances, 3{,}668 artists, 645 genres, 129 instruments, 12 keys,
9 tempo classes, 7 decades. The KG is loaded into GraphDB for validation and
streamed into a PyTorch Geometric \texttt{HeteroData} object for learning. (KG
design and SPARQL validation are detailed in the companion Knowledge Graphs
report; here we focus on the learning stack.)

\paragraph{Splits.} A single user-level stratified split (by primary genre and
decade, 2{,}186 strata) gives 1{,}943{,}681 train, 348{,}054 validation, and
559{,}806 test interactions over 284{,}864 users and 6{,}054 evaluated songs.

\section{Models and Technical Approach}
\label{sec:models}

\subsection{Symbolic-Feature Autoencoder}
A fully connected autoencoder compresses the standardised 1{,}526-dimensional
symbolic vector to a 128-dimensional bottleneck. The encoder is
$1526\!\to\!512\!\to\!256\!\to\!128$ with a symmetric decoder, each hidden layer
followed by ReLU and dropout~0.2. The bottleneck embedding serves both as the
track node feature in the GNN and as the item/user representation in the hybrid
baseline. It minimises reconstruction MSE.

\subsection{Knowledge-Graph Embeddings}
Our KG is dominated by \emph{asymmetric} relations (a user listens to a track, not
the reverse), which real-valued translation models cannot represent. \textbf{RotatE}
encodes each relation as a complex rotation ($h\circ r\approx t$), naturally
capturing asymmetry, inversion, and \emph{compositional} chains (e.g.\ a decade
predecessor/successor traversal); it is our primary initialiser. \textbf{ComplEx}
achieves asymmetry by conjugation and is retained for comparison. Both are trained
on 807{,}955 triples (listening edges excluded to prevent target leakage),
entity\_dim~128, producing 256-dimensional vectors for all 304{,}110 entities.

\subsection{From KG to Learnable Tensors}
Each ontology class becomes a node type and each predicate a typed directed edge.
Track nodes are initialised with the 384-dimensional concatenation of the 128-dim
autoencoder embedding and the 256-dim RotatE vector; all other node types receive
their 256-dim KG embedding. Because listening events are direct
\texttt{user}$\to$\texttt{track} edges, user-to-item message passing requires a
single hop. To let the user node receive and emit messages symmetrically, message
passing is performed on the \emph{undirected} graph; this consistency between
training and inference is the fix for the Milestone~2 directed-evaluation artefact.

\subsection{Heterogeneous Graph Transformer}
The HGT~\cite{hu2020hgt} stack uses 2 message-passing layers, 4 attention heads,
hidden dimension 256, and dropout~0.1, learning separate transformation and
attention parameters per (source-type, edge-type, target-type) triple
(see the main-model column of Fig.~\ref{fig:pipeline}). Each \texttt{HGTConv} layer
applies per-node-type Q/K/V projections and per-edge-type message/attention
weights, computes 4-head attention over typed neighbours, aggregates, and passes
through a per-target-type output linear, LayerNorm, ReLU, dropout, and a residual
connection. A per-type MLP head ($256\!\to\!256\!\to\!128$) projects the final
hidden state to a 128-dimensional output that is $\ell_2$-normalised; scoring is
cosine similarity between user and track embeddings with seen items masked.

\paragraph{Loss.} For normalised user embedding $\hat{\mathbf{u}}$ and track
embeddings $\hat{\mathbf{t}}_i$, the logit-adjusted score is
\begin{equation}
  s_i = \tfrac{\hat{\mathbf{u}}^\top\hat{\mathbf{t}}_i}{\tau} + \lambda\log p_i,
\end{equation}
where $\tau{=}0.1$ sharpens the softmax and $p_i$ is the Laplace-smoothed marginal
listen probability. Since $\log p_i<0$, popular tracks are penalised and long-tail
items are surfaced. With soft targets $\tilde{p}_{ui}\propto\log(1+c_{ui})$
(play-count intensity, row-normalised), the objective is the listwise
cross-entropy
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}=-\frac{1}{|B|}\sum_{u\in B}\sum_i
  \tilde{p}_{ui}\,\log\mathrm{softmax}(\mathbf{s}_u)_i .
\end{equation}
Checkpoints are selected by Overall@10 (Eq.~\ref{eq:overall}) on validation.

\subsection{Baselines}
\textbf{MostPopular} ranks unseen items by global play frequency, identically for
all users. \textbf{KNN-CF} is a user--user collaborative filter on the
$\ell_2$-normalised interaction matrix; $k{=}6$ is selected by a validation sweep.
\textbf{XGBoost Hybrid} retrieves 200 candidates per user via autoencoder-profile
cosine similarity and re-ranks with a gradient-boosted LTR model
(\texttt{rank:ndcg@10}) on 300-dimensional feature vectors (128-dim user-profile
$\Vert$ 128-dim track AE $\Vert$ 44 categorical features: one-hot key/mode/tempo
class, target-encoded genre/artist, multi-hot instruments), fusing content and
behaviour without graph structure.

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

We run the following experiments, all on the splits of Sec.~\ref{sec:data}:
\begin{itemize}\setlength\itemsep{1pt}
  \item \textbf{Autoencoder compression}: 200-epoch transductive run over 6{,}054
        tracks; per-epoch MSE monitored.
  \item \textbf{KGE comparison}: RotatE vs.\ ComplEx (200 epochs each) as HGT node
        initialisers.
  \item \textbf{Architecture search}: HGT sweeps over width, heads, layers,
        dropout, learning rate, weight decay, and temperature, ranked by Overall@10
        on validation.
  \item \textbf{Initialisation ablation}: RotatE vs.\ random node features at the
        selected configuration.
  \item \textbf{Debiasing ($\lambda$) ablation}: $\lambda\in\{0,0.1,0.2,0.3,0.5\}$
        to characterise the ranking-versus-coverage trade-off.
  \item \textbf{Multi-$K$ benchmark}: all four models at
        $K\in\{5,10,20,50,100\}$ with paired significance testing.
  \item \textbf{Interpretability}: latent-space clustering (GMM over UMAP) and
        per-edge attention rationales.
\end{itemize}
Training budgets and hardware are summarised in Tab.~\ref{tab:budget}.

\section{Results}

\subsection{Representation Learning Curves}
The autoencoder trains for the full 200 epochs (early stopping never triggers),
with MSE falling from 0.964 to 0.616, steepest in the first $\sim$75 epochs
(Fig.~\ref{fig:ae}). The residual MSE reflects the aggressive $12\times$
dimensionality reduction. RotatE converges to a lower absolute loss than ComplEx
(0.356 vs.\ 0.381; Fig.~\ref{fig:kge}), consistent with its better fit to the
asymmetric/compositional relations in the KG.

\subsection{HGT Training, Selection, and Ablations}
Figure~\ref{fig:hgt} summarises the HGT. Training and validation listwise loss
decrease smoothly with no divergence (panel a); validation Overall@10 and its
components rise and plateau, with the best checkpoint at epoch~240
(Overall@10~0.367; panel b). The debiasing ablation (panel c) reveals a clean
monotonic trade-off: increasing $\lambda$ lowers NDCG/Recall but raises coverage
and lowers popularity bias. Concretely (validation, selected configuration):
$\lambda{=}0$ gives Recall@10~0.123, NDCG@10~0.069, coverage~1.6\%, whereas
$\lambda{=}0.5$ gives Recall@10~0.070, NDCG@10~0.038, coverage~18.9\% and the best
Overall@10 (0.246); checkpoint selection on the composite score therefore lands at
$\lambda{=}0.5$. The initialisation ablation (panel d) shows that, \emph{unlike our
Milestone~2 finding}, RotatE now beats random initialisation on the composite
objective (Overall@10~0.372 vs.\ 0.320), driven by markedly higher coverage
(0.696 vs.\ 0.406); random init retains a small NDCG edge (0.088 vs.\ 0.067),
confirming that KGE structure mainly helps diversity rather than raw ranking.

\subsection{Benchmark}
Table~\ref{tab:benchmark} reports all models at $K{=}10$ over the full
284{,}864-user test set, and Table~\ref{tab:multik} the multi-$K$ sweep.
\textbf{KNN-CF} attains the best Overall Score (0.421), combining competitive
NDCG@10 (0.106) with 91.6\% coverage. \textbf{The retrained HGT is a close
second} (Overall~0.399): it \emph{beats KNN-CF on per-user Recall@10}
(0.105 vs.\ 0.103, $p<10^{-5}$), reaches 79.7\% coverage, and has the
\emph{lowest popularity bias of all models} (0.053 vs.\ 0.130 for KNN-CF).
MostPopular leads raw NDCG@10 (0.114) but recommends from only 0.6\% of the
catalogue (Overall~0.148). XGBoost reaches 66.7\% coverage but a retrieval
bottleneck caps NDCG@10 at 0.044. All pairwise Recall/NDCG differences are
statistically significant ($p<0.001$ except HGT vs.\ KNN-CF on Recall,
$p\approx9\!\times\!10^{-6}$); Figure~\ref{fig:sig} shows per-user distributions
with set-level significance bars. The multi-$K$ sweep (Tab.~\ref{tab:multik})
confirms the ordering: HGT's Overall Score climbs from 0.372 at $K{=}5$ to 0.474
at $K{=}100$ and overtakes KNN-CF for $K\!\geq\!20$ as its coverage and low
popularity bias compound.

\subsection{Latent Space and Interpretability}
Projecting all nine node types from the HGT's 128-dimensional output space to 2-D
with UMAP (Fig.~\ref{fig:latentall}) reveals a strongly \emph{type-structured}
geometry: in most cases the model places each node type in its own region. Artists
and the attribute/concept nodes --- genres, keys, modes, tempo classes,
instruments, and decades --- collapse into a single, well-separated cluster (GMM
cluster~13), distinct from the user--track mass. Within that mass, however, users
and tracks are deliberately \emph{not} pulled apart: a dense track region sits
alongside the user clusters and individual users fall right among tracks. We read
this user--track proximity not as noise but as the geometric signature of the
training objective --- recommendation scores are cosine similarities between user
and track embeddings, so the model is rewarded for embedding each user into the
track manifold near the items it would recommend. In other words, on top of the
clean content/structure separation, the HGT learns a collaborative-filtering-like
placement of users among the tracks they are predicted to like.

  \caption{All-node-type HGT latent space (UMAP). Left: by node type; right: 20
  BIC-selected GMM components. Artists and attribute/concept nodes form a

Restricting the projection to users alone and fitting a BIC-optimal Gaussian
Mixture yields 20 components (Fig.~\ref{fig:latent}) that form coherent
\emph{listener archetypes} described by their dominant genre, tempo class, mode,
and decade (e.g.\ ``rock$\,\cdot\,$Allegro$\,\cdot\,$2000s'',
``dance pop$\,\cdot\,$Andante$\,\cdot\,$2000s'',
``rock$\,\cdot\,$Allegro$\,\cdot\,$1970s''). That such structure emerges at all is
evidence that HGT message passing propagates musically meaningful context rather
than merely fitting interactions; these archetypes also seed the cold-start persona
recommender of Sec.~\ref{sec:coldstart}.

  GMM components, each an interpretable listening archetype.}
  \label{fig:latent}

\paragraph{Caveats on the latent analysis.} Two limitations temper this reading.
First, the embeddings inhabit a 128-dimensional space whose spatial arrangement is
not directly comprehensible to humans; the 2-D UMAP plots are a non-isometric,
lossy projection that distorts true distances and densities, so they are a
qualitative aid rather than a faithful picture of the latent geometry (the GMM is
fit in the full 128-D space --- UMAP is used only for display). Second, for the
selected full-covariance model the BIC decreased monotonically up to the largest
component count we tested (20) without reaching a minimum (Fig.~\ref{fig:bic},
Appendix), so the 20-component choice is a boundary selection rather than a true
optimum. We capped the sweep at 20 deliberately: a cold-start persona system that
must slot a new user into one of \emph{more} than twenty profiles defeats its own
purpose, and our aim here was chiefly to confirm that the continuous embeddings
cluster meaningfully in high dimensions rather than to identify the globally
optimal mixture. Extending the component sweep or adopting a different clustering
method (e.g.\ agglomerative/hierarchical clustering) is left to future work.

Because HGT learns typed attention, we can attribute each recommendation to
specific neighbours and edge types. Figure~\ref{fig:rationale} contrasts three
attribution views --- raw attention, score-delta ablation, and integrated
gradients --- for one recommendation, showing that shared instruments and a
matching mode/genre drive the suggested track. Such rationales are produced at no
extra modelling cost and are unavailable to the CF baselines.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.92\linewidth]{rationale_compare.png}
  \caption{Three explanation views of a single HGT recommendation (attention,
  score-delta, integrated gradients), tracing the suggestion to shared instruments
  and a matching genre/mode.}
  \label{fig:rationale}
\end{figure}

At the population level (Fig.~\ref{fig:population}, Appendix), HGT attains by far
the highest user--recommendation cosine alignment in its own latent space
(mean~0.004 vs.\ $\leq$0.001 for MostPopular) while distributing hits across the
hit-rate-vs-cosine plane more evenly than the popularity baseline. A per-user
contrastive study (Tab.~\ref{tab:cases}, Appendix) further shows cases where the
HGT is the \emph{only} model to score hits (e.g.\ user~15906: 4 hits vs.\ 0 for all
baselines), supporting the view that the graph model surfaces content-consistent
items the behavioural baselines miss. For the XGBoost hybrid, gain- and SHAP-based
attribution (Figs.~\ref{fig:xgbimp}--\ref{fig:shap}, Appendix) show that the track
autoencoder dimensions dominate ranking (mean$|$SHAP$|$ 0.032 vs.\ 0.016 for the
user profile and 0.007 for categorical features), with \texttt{key=G} the single
most influential categorical feature --- confirming that content embeddings, not
metadata, carry the hybrid's signal.

\subsection{Cold-Start Persona Recommendation}
\label{sec:coldstart}
Every model evaluated above needs interaction history, so none can serve a brand-new
user --- the cold-start problem. We exploit a property unique to the HGT: because
attribute nodes (genres, tempo classes, modes, decades, instruments), tracks, and
users all share \emph{one} embedding space, a new user can be placed in that space
from a few declared preferences, with no retraining. The user selects musical
attributes; we average the chosen anchor embeddings into a taste vector and offer
three inference paths. (i)~\textbf{Persona snap}: assign the vector to the nearest
of 20 \emph{listener archetypes} --- the centroids of a users-only GMM fit on the
HGT latent space (Sec.~\ref{sec:results}, Tab.~\ref{tab:personas}). (ii)~\textbf{k-NN
user blend}: form a bespoke point on the user manifold as a similarity-weighted
softmax mean of the $k{=}15$ nearest real users, with neighbour dispersion as a
confidence signal. (iii)~\textbf{Inductive HGT}: insert a temporary user node into
the undirected graph, feed it a KGE-space feature from the picked attributes, wire
it to a few taste-matched seed tracks, and run a single forward pass; this yields an
inductive user embedding \emph{and} genuine softmax attention, so the same explainer
used in Sec.~\ref{sec:results} produces faithful per-edge rationales for the new
user. All three are pure inference and are exposed through an interactive widget
(taste form $\rightarrow$ ranked recommendations $\rightarrow$ ``why'' panel),
turning the learned latent geometry into a usable cold-start recommender.

\section{Discussion: Known Issues and Limitations}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{Resolving the Milestone~2 evaluation artefact.} Our Milestone~2 report
recorded a near-zero HGT benchmark caused by a train/inference mismatch: the model
was trained with undirected message passing but evaluated on the directed graph,
which prevents messages from reaching user nodes at inference. Re-running inference
on the undirected graph --- matching training --- recovers the full performance
(Tab.~\ref{tab:dirfix}): Overall Score rises from 0.208 to 0.399 and Recall@10 from
0.001 to 0.105. This single protocol fix, not any architectural change, is what
moves the HGT from last to second place; all numbers in this report use the
consistent undirected protocol.

\begin{table}[t]
\centering\footnotesize
\caption{HGT under directed vs.\ consistent undirected inference (test set). The
directed/undirected mismatch alone, not model capacity, explains the Milestone~2
collapse.}
\label{tab:dirfix}
\setlength{\tabcolsep}{5pt}
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.1}
\begin{tabular}{lccc}
\toprule
\textbf{Metric} & \textbf{Undirected} & \textbf{Directed} & \textbf{$\Delta$}\\
\midrule
Recall@10        & 0.105 & 0.001 & 0.104\\
NDCG@10          & 0.097 & 0.002 & 0.095\\
HitRate@10       & 0.175 & 0.003 & 0.172\\
Coverage@10      & 0.796 & 0.041 & 0.755\\
Popularity Bias  & 0.090 & 0.009 & 0.082\\
Overall Score    & 0.399 & 0.208 & 0.192\\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\paragraph{Offline evaluation is an incomplete proxy.} The composite score exposes
a genuine ranking-versus-diversity tension. MostPopular maximises NDCG by
exploiting long-tail play-count structure yet ignores 99.4\% of the catalogue,
while HGT and KNN-CF trade a little NDCG for near-complete coverage. Crucially,
absence of a track from a user's history does not mean dislike; a held-out
implicit-feedback test set therefore under-credits models (like HGT) that surface
unheard but plausibly-liked items. This is consistent with HGT's high latent-space
alignment to user profiles despite a modest hit rate, and motivates a user study.

\paragraph{Retrieval bottleneck.} The XGBoost hybrid's low NDCG is a first-stage
ceiling from 200-candidate retrieval, not a re-ranking failure; an approximate
nearest-neighbour candidate stage is the highest-value fix.

\paragraph{KGE contributes diversity, not ranking.} RotatE initialisation now
improves the composite score via coverage, but random init still wins on raw NDCG,
so the KGE structural prior is partly subsumed by HGT's own message passing. A
controlled study of frozen vs.\ fine-tuned KGE and of corpus composition is
warranted.

\paragraph{Limited search.} Hyperparameters were chosen by a coarse one-axis sweep
under time pressure; a joint Bayesian search over width, depth, heads, $\tau$, and
$\lambda$ could shift the operating point.

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}
\label{sec:conclusion}
We built a deep pipeline --- symbolic autoencoder, transductive KG embeddings, and
a heterogeneous graph transformer with a popularity-debiased listwise loss --- for
content- and preference-aware music recommendation. Evaluating the HGT with
message passing consistent with training (undirected) fixes the Milestone~2
evaluation artefact, and a retuned architecture lifts it further: the model is now
a strong second overall (0.399), beats KNN-CF on Recall@10,
overtakes it on Overall Score for $K\!\geq\!20$, and achieves the lowest popularity
bias of any model while remaining interpretable. Future work includes ANN-based
candidate generation for the hybrid, a factorial ablation isolating KG structure /
symbolic features / debiasing, graph-collaborative baselines
(LightGCN~\cite{he2020lightgcn}, KGCN~\cite{wang2019kgcn}), a broader KGE
comparison, joint hyperparameter optimisation, a larger-component or hierarchical
clustering of the latent space, and an online user study to address the
offline-evaluation gap.
